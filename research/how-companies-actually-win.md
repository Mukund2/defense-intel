# How Small Defense Companies Actually Win Government Contracts

*Deep research — March 2026. Sourced from defense industry practitioners, capture management experts, Shipley methodology, GovCon blogs, Lohfeld Consulting data, and federal acquisition regulations.*

---

## 1. The Actual Capture Process

The real process is nothing like "find opportunity, write proposal, submit." Winning companies treat capture as a **12-24 month campaign** that starts long before the RFP drops. Here's what actually happens:

### The Shipley 7-Phase Business Development Lifecycle

The industry-standard framework (used by virtually every prime and serious mid-tier):

| Phase | Name | What Actually Happens | Timeline |
|-------|------|----------------------|----------|
| 0 | **Market Segmentation** | Evaluate marketplace, identify segments where you can win. Not "what's available" but "where do we have right to win." | Ongoing |
| 1 | **Long-Range Positioning** | Build relationships with agencies 2-5 years out. Publish white papers. Attend industry days. Become known. | Years out |
| 2 | **Opportunity Assessment** | Qualify specific opportunities. Go/no-go decisions. This is where most small companies fail — they chase everything. | 18-24 months pre-RFP |
| 3 | **Capture Planning** | The critical phase. Build capture plan, run Black Hat reviews, develop solution, build team, engage customer. | 12-18 months pre-RFP |
| 4 | **Proposal Planning** | Outline proposal, draft executive summary, update pricing — **before the RFP drops**. | 6-12 months pre-RFP |
| 5 | **Proposal Development** | Actually write the proposal. If you're starting here, you've already lost. | 30-90 days (RFP response window) |
| 6 | **Post-Submittal** | Orals, discussions, debriefs, protests. Solidify customer relationship. | Post-submission |

Shipley documents **96 specific capture and proposal activities** across these 7 phases. Companies that execute them have dramatically higher win rates.

### The Numbers That Matter

- Companies that start capture **6+ months before RFP release** have win rates **above 60%**
- Companies that wait until the RFP drops see win rates **below 20%**
- **80% of the customer's buying decision is already made before proposals are submitted** (APMP data)
- High-performing companies spend **60% of their B&P budget on capture** and only 40% on proposal writing
- The average government solicitation receives only **4.5 bids**, and ~40% receive **fewer than 3 qualified bids**

### Gate Reviews: The Investment Decision Framework

Winning companies use decision gates at each phase transition — formal reviews where leadership decides whether to continue investing in a pursuit. This prevents the #1 small business mistake: chasing opportunities they can't win and burning B&P budget on losers.

**The key gate questions:**
- Do we have customer access and intelligence?
- Do we understand the competitive landscape?
- Do we have a credible win strategy?
- Can we price competitively and still make money?
- What's our Pwin (probability of win)?

### Probability of Win (Pwin) Scoring

Serious capture teams calculate Pwin using a weighted factor model:

**Pwin = (A x w1) + (B x w2) + (C x w3) + ... (N x wn)**

Key factors scored:
- Customer & Relationship (access, intimacy, understanding of "hot buttons")
- Competitive Landscape (incumbent advantage, known competitors, discriminators)
- Technical Fit (solution compliance, innovations, risks)
- Past Performance (relevance, recency, CPARS quality)
- Key Personnel (named resumes, availability, labor mix)
- Price-to-Win / Cost Realism (position vs. budget, wrap rates, risk)
- Teaming & SB Strategy (partners, socioeconomic plan, workshare)
- Compliance & Proposal Risk (RFP interpretation, volume plan, color reviews)
- Capture Maturity (gate artifacts, customer calls, Black Hat complete)

Best practice: calibrate against historical wins/losses with transparent rationale. Recalculate Pwin at every gate.

## 2. Why Companies Lose

The top reasons, ranked by frequency and impact:

### Tier 1: Fatal Errors (Instant Disqualification)

1. **Non-compliance with solicitation requirements.** Missing forms, ignoring formatting rules, missing page limits, late submission. This is embarrassingly common. Even sophisticated companies get tripped up by minor compliance details.

2. **Failure to answer the mail.** The proposal doesn't actually answer what the RFP asked. Companies write what they want to say instead of what the evaluator needs to read. If the RFP says "describe your approach to X" and you write a capabilities overview, you lose.

3. **Unrealistic pricing.** Either too low (fails price realism review — evaluators know you can't deliver at that price) or too high (priced yourself out). Government evaluators are sophisticated about pricing.

### Tier 2: Structural Disadvantages (Hard to Overcome)

4. **No pre-RFP engagement.** Showed up cold. Didn't shape requirements. Didn't build relationships. You're reading the same RFP as everyone else with zero inside knowledge of what the customer actually wants.

5. **Weak or absent past performance.** The chicken-and-egg problem. You need past performance to win contracts, but you need contracts to get past performance. Small businesses with one contract and one negative CPARS rating are at 100% negative — a large firm with the same issue is at 2%.

6. **Bait-and-switch on personnel.** Proposing star players you don't actually have committed, or who leave after award. Government evaluators are wise to this. One of the fastest ways to get protested.

### Tier 3: Execution Failures (Preventable)

7. **Vague proposals.** Doesn't explain *how* you'll accomplish requirements. Leaves evaluators with questions. Generalities instead of specifics. "We will leverage our expertise to deliver..." means nothing.

8. **No homework on the customer.** Firms that don't understand the agency's mission, organization, pain points, and priorities write generic proposals. Evaluators can tell immediately.

9. **Bad teaming arrangements.** Teaming partners who don't complement your weaknesses, or who are actually your competitors on other bids. Joint ventures formed hastily without real integration.

10. **Misrepresentation.** Inflating experience, misstating certifications, hiding adverse past performance. Gets caught. Gets you blacklisted.

### The Fundamental Problem for Small Companies

Small businesses don't give capture the time and attention required. They find an opportunity 30 days before it's due, scramble to write a proposal, and wonder why they lost. They're competing against companies that have been working the opportunity for 18 months.

## 3. Pre-RFP Shaping

**This is the single highest-leverage activity in government contracting.** Companies that shape the RFP before it drops have an enormous advantage — they're essentially writing the test they're going to take.

### What Shaping Actually Looks Like

**12-24 months before RFP:**
- Identify upcoming requirements through budget documents, 5-year plans, existing contract expirations
- Map stakeholders: contracting officers, program managers, technical leads, end users
- Begin relationship building — not "selling," but understanding their problems

**RFI/Sources Sought Response (6-18 months out):**
- Respond to every relevant RFI with substantive, thoughtful content
- Use RFI responses to **plant ideas** about requirements that favor your solution
- Demonstrate thought leadership on the agency's challenges

**White Papers and Capability Briefings:**
- Publish white papers aligned with agency challenges
- Request meetings to present innovative approaches
- These are shared internally by agency champions and can influence requirements writing

**Industry Days (whenever they happen):**
- Attend every relevant industry day
- Ask pointed questions that subtly highlight requirements your competitors can't meet
- Use one-on-one sessions to build relationships with program staff

**Pre-solicitation meetings with contracting officers:**
- Discuss your capabilities, approach, and understanding of requirements
- This is **legal and encouraged** before formal solicitation
- After solicitation, communications become restricted

### How Much Does Shaping Matter?

The data is overwhelming:

- **As much as 80% of the customer's buying decision is already made before proposals are submitted** (APMP)
- Companies that engage 12-24 months early have fundamentally different win rates than those who respond cold
- "Influence is strongest in the pre-RFP stage where you engage with program managers, contracting officers, and stakeholders to understand their needs and position your company as a trusted advisor"
- The capture period for a typical contract lasts **12-24 months** — "It takes that long to convince the customer they need you and not someone else"

### What's Legal

Pre-solicitation, contractors **may**:
- Discuss product/service qualities and characteristics
- Provide information necessary for informed agency decisions
- Engage in technical discussions about unsolicited proposals
- Make capability presentations
- Respond to RFIs, Sources Sought notices, and Industry Day invitations

What's **not** allowed: using appropriated funds to influence contracting decisions, or communicating about specific solicitations after formal restrictions kick in.

### The Shaping Playbook

1. **Offer white papers** that align your differentiators with the agency's stated needs
2. **Respond to RFIs** with requirements language that favors your solution
3. **Attend industry days** and ask questions that highlight gaps only you can fill
4. **Brief program managers** on innovative approaches (before solicitation)
5. **Help the government write better requirements** — they'll include capabilities they've seen demonstrated

## 4. Proposal Evaluation — How Evaluators Actually Score

### The Two Worlds: LPTA vs. Best Value Tradeoff

**LPTA (Lowest Price Technically Acceptable):**
- Binary: your technical proposal is either "acceptable" or "unacceptable"
- Lowest price among acceptable proposals wins. Period.
- No tradeoffs — they can't pay more for a better technical solution
- Appropriate for commodity services, simple requirements, minimal risk
- **If it's LPTA, you compete on price. Full stop.**

**Best Value Tradeoff:**
- Government evaluates both cost/price and non-cost factors (technical approach, past performance, management, etc.)
- Can award to higher-priced offeror if the technical superiority justifies the premium
- Evaluators weigh strengths and weaknesses across proposals
- Used for complex requirements where "you get what you pay for" matters
- **This is where capture and proposal quality create competitive advantage**

### What Evaluators Actually Do

**The Source Selection Evaluation Board (SSEB):**
- Typically 3-7 government employees (technical experts, contracting staff)
- Each evaluator independently reads and scores assigned volumes
- They use **only** the evaluation criteria stated in the RFP — nothing else
- They score on a defined rating scale (Outstanding/Good/Acceptable/Marginal/Unacceptable or similar)
- They document strengths, weaknesses, deficiencies, and risks

**What they're looking for:**
- **Compliance first.** Did you answer everything asked? In the format required?
- **Specificity.** HOW will you do this, not just THAT you will?
- **Proof.** Past performance, examples, named personnel, specific tools and processes
- **Risk mitigation.** What could go wrong and how will you handle it?
- **Understanding of their world.** Do you actually understand what they need and why?

**What gets you strengths (the currency of evaluation):**
- Exceeding minimum requirements in ways that benefit the government
- Innovative approaches that reduce risk or improve outcomes
- Exceptional past performance on similar work
- Key personnel with directly relevant experience
- Management approaches that demonstrate mature processes

**What gets you weaknesses or deficiencies:**
- Vague language ("we will leverage our expertise")
- Not explaining WHO does what (labor categories without mapped responsibilities)
- Passive voice that obscures accountability
- Missing or inadequate risk mitigation
- Past performance that isn't relevant to the current requirement

### Common Elimination Triggers

1. **Late submission** — even by seconds. The system timestamps.
2. **Missing mandatory sections or certifications**
3. **Exceeding page limits** — evaluators stop reading at the limit
4. **Failing to address a stated evaluation criterion**
5. **Price that fails realism analysis** — too low to be credible
6. **Organizational conflict of interest** that wasn't disclosed
7. **Key personnel who don't meet minimum qualifications**

### The Debriefing: Learning From Losses

After award, losing offerors can request a debriefing. Smart companies ask:
- What was the basis for each weakness/deficiency assigned?
- Were there solicitation requirements we failed to address?
- How did the agency weight past performance of subs vs. prime?
- Were there specific considerations that precluded our selection?

Companies that systematically learn from debriefings improve their win rates over time. This is one of the most underused tools in government contracting.

## 5. Pricing Strategy

### The Wrap Rate: The Hidden Battleground

A **wrap rate** is the multiplier applied to base labor costs that accounts for all indirect costs:

**Wrap Rate = (Direct Labor + Fringe + Overhead + G&A) / Direct Labor**

Example: If your direct labor rate is $50/hour and your wrap rate is 2.0, your loaded rate is $100/hour, plus fee.

**Typical components:**
- **Fringe benefits** (health insurance, PTO, payroll taxes): 25-40%
- **Overhead** (facilities, equipment, management, IT): 40-100%+
- **G&A** (corporate functions, BD, admin): 10-25%
- **Fee/Profit**: 8-15% typically

The difference between winning and losing often comes down to **a few percentage points on the wrap rate.** Large primes carry heavy overhead; lean small companies can undercut them significantly.

### Price-to-Win (PTW) Methodology

PTW is the strategic process of estimating the price point that will most likely win a contract. It's not just "be the cheapest" — it's intelligence-driven pricing.

**Steps in a real PTW analysis:**

1. **Research the government's budget.** What can they actually spend? Check budget documents, prior year spending, FPDS data.

2. **Analyze competitor pricing.** Use FOIA'd rates, GSA Schedule rates, IDIQ rates, and salary surveys to estimate competitor wrap rates and labor costs.

3. **Map labor categories.** Match your labor categories to salary surveys (Bureau of Labor Statistics, compensation databases) and to competitor categories.

4. **Calculate bottom-up burden.** For each labor category, calculate your loaded rate including all indirect costs.

5. **Identify the price band.** Where does the government budget, your cost floor, and competitor pricing intersect?

6. **Adjust strategically.** Reduce fee to be more competitive. Adjust labor mix. Consider teaming partners with lower rates.

### Small Company Pricing Advantages

- **Lower overhead.** No 10-story headquarters in Rosslyn. No army of corporate staff.
- **Lower fringe.** Potentially (though competitive benefits help recruiting).
- **More aggressive fee.** Willing to take lower margins to build past performance.
- **Flexibility on labor mix.** Can propose more junior staff with senior oversight.
- **Teaming.** Add a partner with better rates on specific labor categories.

### Pricing Mistakes That Kill Bids

- **Pricing in the dark.** Not researching competitor rates or government budgets.
- **Cost-plus mentality** when the evaluation is LPTA.
- **Understaffing.** Proposing too few people to be credible — evaluators know what it takes.
- **Fee too high.** Government evaluators compare fee percentages across proposals.
- **Not accounting for price realism.** If your price is significantly below the government estimate, you'll face scrutiny about whether you can actually perform.

## 6. Incumbent Advantage

### The Numbers

- **Incumbents win 70-75% of recompetes** in full-and-open competition
- On **small business set-asides**, incumbent win rate drops to **50-55%**
- **25-30% of recompetes go to new contractors** — on $180B in annual recompetes, that's **$45-54B changing hands**
- Challenger win rate on recompetes: **~38%** (vs. only ~12% on net-new competitive solicitations)

This means recompetes are actually the **best hunting ground for challengers** — better odds than purely new competitions.

### Why Incumbents Win

- **Knowledge asymmetry.** They know the work, workforce, agency culture, and unwritten requirements.
- **Relationship depth.** Years of daily interaction with program staff.
- **CPARS ratings.** They have directly relevant past performance that you can't match.
- **Switching costs.** Government fears disruption. Continuity is safe.
- **Requirement shaping.** They've been influencing the follow-on requirements for years.

### Why Incumbents Lose (When They Do)

- **Complacency.** "Love is not an evaluation criterion in federal procurements." Incumbents mistake silence for approval and stop innovating.
- **Inflated pricing.** Costs spiral through salary creep and increasing indirect rates.
- **Stale staffing.** Same people, same approaches, no fresh ideas.
- **Poor CPARS.** Even one bad rating creates vulnerability.
- **Personnel turnover.** Key people leave and the incumbent can't replace them with equivalent talent.

### The Challenger Playbook for Displacing an Incumbent

Successful challengers work **all four levers simultaneously**:

1. **Relationship (Level the Playing Field).** Engage the customer early and often. Don't let the incumbent control the narrative. Build your own relationships with PMs, COs, and end users.

2. **Requirements (Shape the Recompete).** Push for requirements that favor fresh approaches. Highlight capability gaps in the current contract. Introduce innovations the incumbent hasn't offered.

3. **Pricing (Sharpen the Pencil).** Propose tighter indirect rates, less expensive personnel mix, and aggressive fee. Incumbents often have bloated pricing.

4. **Technical (Offer What's Next).** Don't just match the incumbent — offer the next generation. Modern tools, better processes, demonstrable improvements.

**Critical insight:** "Displacing an incumbent is not a proposal problem; it is a capture problem that the proposal reflects." You win or lose the recompete in the 12-24 months before the RFP, not in the 30-day proposal window.

### When NOT to Chase a Recompete

- The incumbent has excellent CPARS and strong customer relationships
- You have zero customer access and can't get any
- The requirements are identical to the current contract (incumbent advantage maximized)
- You can't price competitively without losing money
- The recompete is sole-sourced or has limited competition

## 7. Teaming and Subcontracting

### Why Teaming Matters

Teaming is often the **difference between being eligible and not.** Key reasons to team:

- **Cover capability gaps.** You have the tech but not the cleared facility. Your partner has the facility.
- **Meet set-aside requirements.** A large firm can pursue small business set-asides through a Mentor-Protege Joint Venture.
- **Aggregate past performance.** Combined team has stronger relevant experience.
- **Reduce risk in evaluators' eyes.** A known team with complementary strengths is lower risk than a single unproven company.
- **Meet subcontracting plan requirements.** Large business primes must meet small business subcontracting goals.

### The Three Teaming Mechanisms

**1. Teaming Agreements (CTAs/Teaming Arrangements)**
- One company is prime, others are subcontractors
- The prime holds the contract and is responsible to the government
- Subs perform allocated work under the prime's direction
- Simplest structure — no new legal entity required
- Used for: most competitive bids

**2. Joint Ventures (JVs)**
- New legal entity formed by two or more companies
- Requires SBA approval, separate UEI/CAGE code, new SAM registration
- Both parties share risk and reward
- Used for: when neither partner can win alone, or to leverage set-aside status

**3. Mentor-Protege Joint Ventures**
- A large business (mentor) and small business (protege) form a JV
- The JV qualifies as small business based on the protege's status
- **This is how large companies access small business set-aside contracts**
- Protege gets business development help, technical assistance, and access to the mentor's resources
- Mentor gets access to set-aside contracts and credit toward small business subcontracting goals
- DoD's Mentor-Protege Program (MPP) has run for 35+ years and is now permanent

### Finding and Forming Teams

**Where to find partners:**
- Industry days (specifically structured for teaming)
- SubNet (SBA's subcontracting network)
- GovWin IQ and similar databases
- Industry conferences (AUSA, Sea-Air-Space, etc.)
- Direct outreach to companies with complementary capabilities

**What makes a good teaming arrangement:**
- Complementary capabilities (not overlapping — that creates friction)
- Compatible corporate cultures and working styles
- Clear workshare delineation upfront
- Agreed pricing and fee structures before the bid
- Committed key personnel (not "to be determined")
- Written teaming agreement with enforceable terms

**Red flags in teaming:**
- Partner who wants majority workshare but wants you to be prime (they want your set-aside status)
- No commitment on key personnel
- Vague workshare — "we'll figure it out after we win"
- Partner who is teaming with your competitor on the same bid
- Unbalanced risk allocation

### Performance Requirements to Watch

Small businesses on set-aside contracts must perform minimum levels of work themselves — they can't just pass everything to a large sub:
- **Service contracts:** Cannot subcontract more than 50% of the cost to non-similarly situated entities
- This prevents "pass-through" arrangements where the small business is just a front

## 8. The Role of Relationships

### The Honest Answer: Relationships Matter Enormously — But Not How Most People Think

It's not about corruption or backroom deals. It's about **intelligence and trust.**

**Why relationships drive wins:**
- The customer tells you **what they actually need** (not just what the RFP says)
- You understand their **pain points, budget constraints, and political dynamics**
- You know **who the evaluators will be** and what they value
- You learn about **upcoming requirements 12-24 months early**
- The customer trusts your solution because they've **seen your thinking** through white papers, briefings, and conversations

**The data point:** Relationship-focused firms see **30%+ higher success rates** in competitive bidding.

### The Relationship Matrix

Winning capture teams maintain a relationship matrix targeting **3-4 contacts at each organizational level:**

| Level | Who | What They Want | How to Engage |
|-------|-----|---------------|---------------|
| Senior Executives | SES, General Officers, Political Appointees | Strategic insights, risk reduction | White papers, executive briefings, industry events |
| Program Managers | PMs, Deputy PMs, Technical Directors | Operational solutions, mission success | Capability demos, problem-solving discussions |
| Technical Leads | Engineers, Analysts, End Users | Peer expertise, tools that work | Technical exchanges, prototype demos |
| Contracting Officers | COs, Contract Specialists, SBLOs | Compliance, fair competition, meeting goals | Professional interactions, responsive communications |

### Small Business Liaison Officers (SBLOs)

An underused resource. SBLOs are government employees specifically tasked with facilitating small business participation. They:
- Can serve as **relationship brokers** and make introductions
- Are incentivized to help small businesses win (agencies have small business goals)
- Have broad networks across their agency
- Can point you to upcoming opportunities before formal announcement

### What's Legal vs. What Crosses the Line

**Absolutely fine:**
- Meeting with program managers to discuss capabilities (pre-solicitation)
- Attending industry days and one-on-one sessions
- Responding to RFIs and Sources Sought notices
- Publishing relevant white papers and sharing them
- Attending conferences where government staff speak

**Gets into gray area:**
- Hiring former government employees (revolving door rules apply)
- Discussing specific procurements after solicitation restrictions kick in
- Providing anything of value to government employees (strict gift rules)

**Never okay:**
- Using appropriated funds to influence contracting decisions
- Obtaining competitor proprietary information through government contacts
- Organizational conflicts of interest without disclosure

## 9. Set-Aside Strategies — The Tactical Playbook

### The Programs

| Program | Eligibility | Sole Source Threshold | Key Advantage |
|---------|------------|----------------------|---------------|
| **8(a)** | 51%+ owned by socially/economically disadvantaged citizens | $4.5M (services) / $7M (manufacturing) | Sole-source awards, mentoring, JV with large firms |
| **SDVOSB** | 51%+ owned by service-disabled veteran | $4.5M (services) / $7M (manufacturing) | VA set-asides, sole-source authority |
| **HUBZone** | Principal office in HUBZone, 35%+ employees reside in HUBZone | $4.5M (services) / $7M (manufacturing) | Price evaluation preference (10%), geographic targeting |
| **WOSB/EDWOSB** | 51%+ owned by women, economically disadvantaged | $4.5M (services) / $7M (manufacturing) | Access to underrepresented industry set-asides |

**No order of precedence** exists among these programs — contracting officers can choose which set-aside to use based on the market.

### The 8(a) Playbook (Most Powerful Program)

**Sole-source is the holy grail:**
- Under $4.5M (services) or $7M (manufacturing), agencies can award directly to an 8(a) firm without competition
- Entity-owned 8(a) participants can receive sole-source contracts above these thresholds (DoD requires justification above $100M, other agencies above $25M)
- This means an agency can literally choose to give you the contract — no proposal competition

**Strategic approach:**
1. Get your 8(a) certification
2. Build relationships with program managers who have upcoming requirements
3. Educate them on 8(a) sole-source authority (many COs don't know the details)
4. Position your capability so the agency wants to direct-award to you
5. Help them build the sole-source justification

**Mentor-Protege amplification:**
- Partner with a large firm as mentor
- Form a JV that qualifies as small
- Now you can chase larger contracts than you could alone
- The mentor provides technical resources, past performance, and staffing depth
- You provide the set-aside status

### Recent FAR Part 19 Changes (2025)

Major shift: **The "Once 8(a), Always 8(a)" rule has been dismantled.** An 8(a) follow-on contract can now be competed under HUBZone, SDVOSB, or WOSB set-asides. This means:
- Companies graduating from 8(a) can leverage other status designations
- More competition on previously locked-up 8(a) follow-on work
- Strategic opportunity to target contracts transitioning between programs

### Set-Aside Statistics

- **63% of all small business awards** included a set-aside in 2022 (up from 52% in 2010)
- Set-asides are increasingly the primary pathway for small businesses
- Businesses with **3+ successful government contracts** are 30% more likely to win subsequent bids

### The Alternative Pathways: OTA, SBIR, and CSO

For companies that don't qualify for traditional set-asides or want additional on-ramps:

**SBIR/STTR:**
- Phased R&D funding (Phase I: ~$150K, Phase II: ~$1M, Phase III: production contracts)
- Phase III is the prize — it's a sole-source production contract with no dollar limit
- Builds past performance and positions you for larger programs

**Other Transaction Authorities (OTA):**
- Exempt from FAR — faster, more flexible
- Used for R&D and prototyping
- Especially attractive to non-traditional defense companies
- Can lead to follow-on production contracts

**The winning formula for startups:**
1. Secure SBIR funding to build credibility
2. Use OTA for rapid prototyping
3. Partner with a PEO to scale into a Program of Record

### Color Team Reviews (The Quality System)

Primes run formal review gates during proposal development:

| Review | When | What Happens |
|--------|------|-------------|
| **Blue Team** | Pre-proposal | Validates capture strategy, win themes, solution architecture. "Are we ready to write?" |

- Team role-plays as competitors
- Each "competitor team" builds a mock proposal strategy

- Identifies vulnerabilities and ghosting opportunities
- Informs PTW and solution differentiation

The primes spend **$500K-$2M per capture** with dedicated teams working 12-24 months. A small company has the founder doing BD at night after delivering on existing contracts.

**The software opportunity is to give a 5-person company the capture intelligence and process discipline of a Lockheed Martin BD team.** Not by replacing humans — by making the humans 10x more effective:

1. **Find the right opportunities** — Don't chase everything. AI-driven pipeline qualification.
2. **Know the competition** — Automated competitive intelligence from public data.
3. **Price to win** — Data-driven pricing based on market benchmarks, not guesses.
4. **Shape before the RFP** — Track pre-RFP engagement opportunities, stakeholder mapping, relationship status.
5. **Write winning proposals** — Compliance checking, evaluation criteria mapping, strength/weakness analysis.
6. **Learn from every bid** — Capture debriefings, track Pwin accuracy, improve over time.
7. **Run the process** — Guided Shipley workflow adapted for small teams. Phase gates without the bureaucracy.
