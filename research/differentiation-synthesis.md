# Final Differentiation Synthesis
*March 11, 2026 — All 4 research streams combined*

---

## The Landscape (Simplified)

The govcon AI space has two types of products:

**"Finders"** — help you discover and track opportunities:
- Usul (defense-focused, AI matching, PEO mapping, $3.3M raised, YC S24)
- GovWin/Deltek ($29K/yr avg, legacy, keyword-based)
- GovTribe, BidPrime, Federal Compass, GovSpend

**"Doers"** — help you write proposals and manage capture:
- GovDash (all-gov, end-to-end BD, $44M raised, YC W22)
- GovSignals (FedRAMP High, proposal writing, 400+ orgs)
- Sweetspot ($720-$3,600/yr, all-gov)

**Nobody is both.** Usul finds but doesn't help you win. GovDash helps you write but isn't defense-specific and doesn't have deep intelligence.

---

## What Usul ACTUALLY Does Well (Updated After Deep Dive)

1. **AI capability matching (1-100 scores)** — not keyword/NAICS, genuinely capability-based
2. **PEO-to-budget-line mapping** — links program element lines to decision-makers with contact info. This is their real structural asset.
3. **Forecasts** — scanning 500M+ transactions, predicting recompetes 24 months out. Positioning as "GovWin Killer."
4. **Mobile "Tinder for contracts" UX** — swipe-based opportunity evaluation that trains the AI
5. **International expansion** — already in 32 NATO nations
6. **Palantir partnership** — credibility + data infrastructure
7. **Shipping velocity** — weekly updates, 11 people, moving fast

**What Usul DOES NOT DO:**
1. No proposal writing or RFP analysis
2. No pricing/bid modeling
3. No compliance navigation for specific opportunities
4. No SBIR/STTR tooling despite targeting startups
5. No post-award contract management
6. No FedRAMP or security certifications
7. No case studies or verified ROI data

**Their tagline says "winning." Their product is "finding."**

## Three Possible Differentiation Strategies (Stress-Tested)

### Strategy A: "The Winning Half" — Pick Up Where Usul Stops

**Thesis:** Usul finds opportunities. We help you win them. Not a competitor — a complement.

**What this looks like:**
- Take a qualified opportunity (from Usul, SAM.gov, anywhere)
- Shred the RFP → compliance matrix → win themes → proposal drafts → pricing strategy
- Incumbent weakness analysis from FPDS data
- Bid/no-bid scoring based on your specific company vs. this specific opportunity
- "Here's exactly what to emphasize, here's the price range, here's what the evaluator cares about"

**Durability test:** MEDIUM. GovDash could add defense depth. Usul could add proposal features (they have the data). But execution is hard — Usul has 11 people across 6 product surfaces and is expanding internationally. They're unlikely to nail proposals anytime soon.

**Market size:** Every company using Usul, GovWin, or SAM.gov is a potential customer. They've already found the opportunity — they need help winning it.

**Risk:** Positioned as a complement, not a platform. Could be acquired or squeezed out.

---

### Strategy B: "SBIR to Production" — Own the Valley of Death

**Thesis:** 95% of SBIR Phase II companies die before reaching production. We're the bridge.

**What this looks like:**
- Company inputs: "I have a Phase II SBIR for [X technology] with [Y agency]"
- Platform maps: which Programs of Record need this technology, which PEOs have unfunded requirements it fills, which STRATFI/TACFI/CATALYST/APFIT programs are accepting applications
- Guides them through: Phase III sole-source justification, PEO sponsorship strategy, transition funding applications
- Tracks: every OTA consortium, every Phase III opportunity, every program with budget for their domain

**Durability test:** HIGH. This is a specific, painful, underserved workflow. Nobody automates it. Companies hire $50K-200K consultants for this. Usul's customers (Firestorm, OneBrief, Overland AI) are PAST this stage — they won't build for it.

**Market size:** ~3,771 unique SBIR awardees per year. $4.3B in annual SBIR funding. Each company would pay significantly for this — it's existential for them. But the total addressable market is narrower than general govcon.

**Risk:** Narrow market. Need to expand beyond SBIR eventually. Could be a wedge into broader defense BD.

---

### Strategy C: "The $149/mo Capture Team" — Affordable Full-Stack for True Underdogs

**Thesis:** 400,000 companies registered in SAM.gov. Most use Excel and SAM.gov. Give them the full BD lifecycle at a price they can afford.

**What this looks like:**
- Opportunity matching (like Usul but at $149/mo, not enterprise pricing)
- Win probability scoring
- Basic capture intelligence (incumbent analysis, competitive landscape)
- RFP analysis + compliance matrix
- Proposal section drafting
- Pipeline tracking (replacing Excel)
- All defense-specific, all self-serve, credit card from day one

**Durability test:** LOW-MEDIUM. This is basically "cheaper Usul + cheaper GovDash." It's a pricing play, not a product differentiation play. Either company could launch a lower tier.

**Market size:** Massive (400K+ companies) but low ARPU ($149/mo = $1,788/yr). Many of these companies are fragile — 60% of winners get less than $100K in contracts.

**Risk:** Race to the bottom. Underdogs have small wallets. High churn. Hard to build a big business at $149/mo from companies that might not exist next year.

---

## Recommendation

**Lead with Strategy A, wedge with Strategy B, price like Strategy C.**

Concretely:
1. **Core product: "The Winning Half"** — you bring us an opportunity (from anywhere), we tell you if you should bid, how to win, and help you write the proposal. Defense-specific capture intelligence + proposal strategy.

2. **Wedge for early traction: SBIR companies** — the most desperate, most underserved segment. They have real budgets (SBIR grants), a burning problem (valley of death), and no tools. Start here, expand outward.

3. **Price for accessibility: $149-349/mo** — self-serve, transparent, credit card. Don't compete with Usul or GovDash on enterprise deals. Own the segment they're leaving behind.

**The pitch:** "Usul helps you find contracts. We help you win them. Starting at $149/mo."

**Why this works:**
- Not competing head-to-head with Usul (complementary, not competitive)
- Not competing with GovDash (defense-specific, self-serve, different customer)
- SBIR wedge gives a focused beachhead with desperate customers
- The "winning" half is where the actual pain is — finding contracts is solved
- Self-serve pricing captures the 400K+ companies nobody else is serving
